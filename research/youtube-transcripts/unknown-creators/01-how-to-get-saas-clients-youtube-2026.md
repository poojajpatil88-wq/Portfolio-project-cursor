**[00:06:50] What Actually Works**

**1. Treat YouTube Like a Search Engine**
For most B2B SaaS companies, the best thing you can do is find specific keywords your ideal clients are already searching for and rank for those keywords. Creating content around these keywords drives qualified traffic — people who are potential clients, not random viewers.

Unlike LinkedIn where you just post and hope the algorithm pushes it, on YouTube you can reliably get traffic from SEO videos.

*Examples of good keywords:*
- "How to set up my SmartLead cold email campaign"

Starting out: find smaller keywords with smaller search volume and lower competition. Don't try to rank for the biggest keywords right away.

**2. Lead With Education, Not Product Features**
Nobody wakes up and searches YouTube for a product demo. They search for specific solutions to the problems they're facing. Your videos need to solve problems and educate your prospect — that's the only way to grow on YouTube.

*Educational content examples:*
- "How to get clients on LinkedIn 2026" (SEO video)
- "How a cold email strategy has a 43% open rate" — everyone watching this already does cold emails or is planning to; they're pre-qualified
- "I sent 1,000 cold emails and added X to the business in 30 days" — even if someone isn't doing cold emails yet, they'll click, watch, and if you structure the video right, you convert them into users/paying clients

**[00:13:03] Execution Models: Three Approaches**

**Option A — Founder on Camera**
- Pros: Highest trust (people buy from people they know), most authentic, lower cost (using existing team), easier to close clients
- Cons: Requires your time on camera, harder to scale if you want to step back, camera confidence takes time to develop, scheduling at scale is hard

**Option B — Hired Presenter / Face of Channel**
- Examples: Clay, Instantly do this
- Pros: Frees up founder's time entirely, publish at higher volume without burnout, professional presenters = high quality from day one, channel not dependent on one person's schedule
- Cons: Lower authenticity, higher ongoing cost (recording + editing), if presenter leaves you may need to rebuild audience trust

**Option C — Creator Partnerships**
- Examples: HeyReach — basically all videos are partnerships with creators in the space
- Pros: Instant access to existing warm audiences, flexible (pay per video, no long-term commitment), if one video per week the cost may not be high
- Cons: Don't fully own the audience, limited control over brand presentation, doesn't compound as much, can get expensive at scale, creator quality and audiences vary
